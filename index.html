Pilot concept

Initial service model

Market: Lake Minnetonka shoreline docks, lifts, and slips.
Product: premium marine gasoline, likely 91 octane non-oxy where supply and rules allow.
Dispatch: pre-scheduled weekday shoreline routes with optional emergency slots.
Positioning: less hassle than marina fueling, cleaner than hauling cans, more reliable than “I'll fill up later.”
Important: Minnesota regulatory approval is a gating issue before launch.

Why this could work

Lake Minnetonka already has multiple marina gas docks, which means the demand for marine fuel is proven. The gap is convenience: scheduled fuel brought to docked boats, especially for owners who value time, keep boats on lifts, or want weekday service before weekend use.

Customer pain

Fueling interrupts lake time

Owners burn time running to gas docks, waiting in no-wake zones, or lugging cans down long docks. A good service removes the trip entirely.

Market signal

The category already exists

Dockside fueling is already marketed in Wisconsin, Michigan, Florida, and other lake markets. That reduces customer education risk.

Go-to-market

Density beats scale early

You do not need statewide coverage. You need one dense lake, repeat customers, clean routing, and predictable gross profit per stop.

How the service should work

A small-start model on Minnetonka should bias toward scheduled runs, known docks, saved payment methods, and repeat customers instead of open-ended, instant dispatch everywhere on the lake.

Customer flow

Simple enough to use from a phone

1) Join the service, 2) save boat location and fuel type, 3) request the next route day, 4) receive confirmation and receipt. The best version feels more like Amazon delivery than calling a marina.

Operations

Start with zones and route days

Split the lake into a few shoreline zones and serve each on predictable days. That keeps miles, idle time, and deadhead travel under control.

Unit economics

This business only works if you treat convenience as the product, not just fuel resale. The cleanest early model is market-rate fuel plus a delivery fee, or market-rate fuel plus a healthy per-gallon markup and a minimum stop size.

Scenario Assumptions Gross profit per stop What it means
Weak order 20 gallons, $1.00/gal margin, no fee $20 Usually too thin once labor, routing, insurance, and wasted miles are counted.
Better order 30 gallons, $1.00/gal margin, $20 delivery fee $50 Starts to look workable if route density is strong and fill time is short.
Strong order 40 gallons, $1.25/gal margin, $25 delivery fee $75 This is the kind of stop economics that can support a small pilot.
Premium urgent 25 gallons, $1.25/gal margin, $45 rush fee $76.25 Useful as an exception, but the business should not rely on rush calls for the base model.
Pricing thought

Do not chase “cheaper than marinas” too hard

Customers are paying for saved time, fewer hassles, and delivered convenience. A race-to-the-bottom fuel price probably kills the model before it gets traction.

Pilot target

Aim for repeat docks

The first 25 to 50 recurring customers matter more than hundreds of random leads. Repeat routes improve economics fast.

Minimums

Protect the stop economics

Minimum gallons, minimum delivery fees, or neighborhood route days are what keep small orders from destroying margins.

Suggested starting price structure

A sensible first pass is to avoid subscriptions and keep the offer easy to understand. The best pilot pricing is usually transparent enough to explain in one sentence.

Recommended pilot

Market fuel price + delivery fee

Example: fuel priced daily, plus a $20 to $30 scheduled dockside fee, waived only above a larger fill threshold. This matches how some dockside operators already signal pricing.

Alternative

Per-gallon markup + minimum order

Example: street-equivalent fuel price plus about $1.25 to $1.50 per gallon, with a 25- or 30-gallon minimum. This is cleaner if customers dislike a visible trip charge.

Big risk to solve first

The biggest question is not demand. It is regulatory permission and operating structure on Lake Minnetonka.

Important

Minnetonka is not a clean green light

Past Lake Minnetonka discussions show that Minnesota fire-code interpretation and LMCD policy were major obstacles for fuel-boat operations, especially gasoline fueling from boat to boat outside approved marine fuel-dispensing facilities.

Next step

Validate legal path before spending

Before building trucks, barges, or branding, verify with the relevant fire, environmental, weights-and-measures, and local lake authorities exactly what delivery model is allowed and what is not.

Customer questions

A landing page should answer the obvious friction points up front so interested boat owners know this is a real service concept.

Do I need to be there when you fuel?

Ideally no. The best model is a saved payment method, saved fueling instructions, and a texted receipt after service.

How is pricing different from a marina gas dock?

The pitch is convenience first. Customers may pay a delivery fee or premium, but they save the time and hassle of leaving their dock to fuel up.

What areas would you serve first?

The launch should start with one tight Lake Minnetonka route where shoreline density and repeat demand are highest, then expand only when the stop economics are proven.
